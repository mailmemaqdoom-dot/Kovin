# The Kovin Principles

This is not a feature. This is the operating system.

Every other document in this repository — every page, every engine, every shared
module — is downstream of the ten principles below. They are not marketing copy
(though [principles.html](principles.html) presents them to customers). They are
the test every decision in this business is meant to pass, on every surface:
**Website, PWA, ERP, Admin Panel, CRM, Consultation, Sales, Technicians,
Inventory, Customer Support, and any future AI system Kovin builds or buys.**

If a new feature, script, or process satisfies a sales target but fails one of
these ten, the principle wins. That is the whole point of writing them down.

---

## I. Clarity before Conversion.

> Never optimise for a sale if it reduces customer understanding.

A confused customer who buys is a worse outcome than a customer who delays
buying because they understand the decision better. Conversion rate is a
lagging indicator of clarity, not a target to chase directly. If an A/B test
shows that *hiding* information increases conversion, that information stays
visible anyway.

**In practice:** no dark patterns, no pre-checked upsells, no copy that implies
urgency that doesn't exist. The [Decision Diary](library.html), the
[Confidence Engine](confidence.html), and [Second Opinion](secondopinion.html)
all exist specifically to slow a purchase down until it's understood.

## II. Recommend only what you would confidently recommend to your own family.

The test for any recommendation — written by a person, generated by a rules
engine, or (eventually) suggested by an AI model — is not "will this convert"
but "would I tell my own father this." [Family Hub](family.html) and
[Life Modes](lifemode.html) exist to make this literal: the same logic that
recommends a phone to a stranger is the logic that recommends one to "Appa &
Amma."

**In practice:** margin, stock levels, and vendor relationships never enter
the recommendation logic. If the honest answer is "buy nothing yet" or "buy it
from someone else," that is the recommendation.

## III. Never hide trade-offs.

> Every recommendation must explain what customers gain and what they give up.

A recommendation without a trade-off is not a recommendation — it's an
advertisement. Every product, every life-mode fit, every "why this and not
that" must name what's being given up, not just what's being gained.

**In practice:** the [Knowledge Graph](knowledgegraph.html)'s `reasonFor()`
always returns a `whyNotAlternative`, never just a `fits` list. If a
trade-off can't be named, the recommendation isn't ready to ship.

## IV. A smaller honest catalogue is more valuable than a larger confusing one.

Catalogue breadth is not a goal. Every SKU added without a clear, distinct
reason to exist makes every other recommendation slightly less trustworthy,
because it raises the odds the customer is shown something irrelevant.

**In practice:** Inventory and purchasing decisions are evaluated by "does
this make the catalogue easier to reason about," not just "will this sell."
The [Elimination Engine](eliminate.html) is built on the assumption that
fewer, well-understood options beat a wall of specs.

## V. Technology should adapt to people's lives. People should never adapt to technology.

The product changes its framing to match the customer's situation — not the
other way around. [Life Modes](lifemode.html) is the literal implementation
of this: the same catalogue, organized around "what's happening in your life"
rather than "what category of device is this."

**In practice:** never ship a feature that requires a customer to learn new
vocabulary, new workflow, or new patience to get value from it. If a feature
needs an onboarding tutorial to make sense, the feature is wrong, not the
tutorial.

## VI. Ownership is more important than purchase. Design experiences for years, not minutes.

The sale is the shortest part of the relationship. [Ownership
Journey](journey.html), [Technology Horizon](horizon.html), and [Technology
Memory](memory.html) exist because the months and years after checkout are
where trust is actually built or lost.

**In practice:** warranty, support, and upgrade-timing guidance are never
treated as a cost center to minimize — they are the product. A repair ticket
or a consultation a year after purchase matters as much as the sale itself.

## VII. Respect attention. Never overload. Never pressure. Never manipulate.

No countdown timers. No fake scarcity. No notification spam. No infinite
scroll designed to extend a session past the point of usefulness. Every
screen in this codebase is built to let a customer leave with an answer, not
to keep them engaged.

**In practice:** every prior feature built in this repository was explicitly
told to avoid fear-based messaging, urgency cues, and pressure tactics — this
was never a one-off instruction, it is this principle, restated each time.

## VIII. Every interaction should leave the customer slightly more knowledgeable.

A successful interaction — a purchase, a consultation, a support call, even a
"no, this isn't for you" — should leave the customer able to explain their own
decision afterward. If a customer can't articulate *why* they bought
something, the interaction failed regardless of revenue.

**In practice:** this is why the [Knowledge Graph](knowledgegraph.html) was
built as a customer-facing explanation engine rather than an internal-only
model — and why [Technology Memory](memory.html) keeps what was learned, not
just what was bought.

## IX. Trust compounds. Never sacrifice long-term trust for short-term revenue.

Every individual decision should be evaluated against its effect on whether
this customer (and the people they tell) trust Kovin *more* a year from now.
A quarter's revenue is recoverable. A reputation for steering people wrong is
not.

**In practice:** [Second Opinion](secondopinion.html) exists to recommend a
competitor's product when that's honestly the better fit, even though it
costs the sale. [Trust Passport](trust.html) exists to make the *result* of
that compounding visible, without ever turning it into a score, level, or
loyalty mechanic — trust is demonstrated, not gamified.

## X. Every recommendation must be explainable. No black-box decisions.

If a recommendation can't be traced back to a specific, statable reason, it
should not be shown. This applies to a human salesperson's gut feeling as
much as to a future machine-learning model: "the algorithm said so" is never
an acceptable answer to a customer who asks why.

**In practice:** the [Knowledge Graph](knowledge-graph.js) is deliberately
rule-based rather than a trained model, specifically so every output traces
to a readable weight or an authored sentence. **Any future AI system Kovin
adopts — for recommendations, support, or sales — must be able to produce a
plain-language reason for its output before it is allowed to influence a
customer-facing decision.** A model that can't explain itself doesn't ship,
regardless of how well it performs in a benchmark.

## How this applies, surface by surface

The principles above are the philosophy. This is the translation — one
concrete obligation per surface this business runs on, today or in the future.

| Surface | What the principles require here |
|---|---|
| **Website / PWA** | Every page in this repository already encodes I–X: no countdowns, every recommendation explainable, ownership-focused content outweighs purchase-focused content. New pages are reviewed against this file before they're considered done. |
| **ERP** | Inventory, pricing, and fulfillment logic must never silently bias toward higher-margin SKUs in what gets recommended or shown first (II, IV). Stock data should make the catalogue *more* honest (e.g., surfacing "limited availability" truthfully), never used to manufacture urgency (VII). |
| **Admin Panel** | Whoever manages products and content needs the same trade-off and explainability fields the customer sees (III, X) — an admin should not be able to publish a recommendation with no stated reason or no named trade-off. |
| **CRM** | Customer history should be used to make guidance better, never to identify "high-pressure-tolerant" customers or optimize manipulation (VII). A customer's record should make them easier to help, not easier to upsell. |
| **Consultation** | The WhatsApp/human conversation is where these principles are easiest to violate under pressure to close — and most important to hold. A consultation that ends in "buy nothing yet, here's why" is a successful consultation (I, II, IX). |
| **Sales** | Compensation structures must not reward staff for selling against a customer's stated need. A sale that required hiding a trade-off is a process failure, not a win (III, IX). |
| **Technicians** | Every repair or service interaction should leave the customer understanding what was actually wrong, not just "it's fixed now" (VIII). Service notes should be explainable to the customer, not just internal shorthand (X). |
| **Inventory** | Breadth for its own sake is a liability, not an asset (IV). A SKU that can't be confidently recommended to family, or whose trade-offs can't be clearly stated, is a candidate for removal regardless of margin. |
| **Customer Support** | Support exists to make the customer more capable, not just to close tickets (VIII). Resolution time matters less than whether the customer leaves understanding their own device better than before. |
| **Future AI** | Any AI system — recommendation, support, sales, or otherwise — inherits every principle above, with X enforced as a hard gate: no AI-generated customer-facing recommendation ships without a human-readable reason attached. This file is the system prompt that future AI work is briefed against, not a retrofit. |

---

*This file should be read before any new feature is designed, and revisited
when an existing feature is changed. If a change to this codebase would
violate one of the ten principles above, that is a reason to stop and ask,
not a reason to proceed quietly.*
